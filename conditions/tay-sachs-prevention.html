Prevention Timeline

Key milestones in the fight against Tay-Sachs disease

1970

The Catalyst Moment

Dr. Michael Kaback at Johns Hopkins works with two families affected by Tay-Sachs: the Gershowitzes and the Zeigers. Bob Zeiger, a pediatric intern, faces the devastating diagnosis of his son Michael. This personal connection drives Kaback to develop the first newborn testing protocol.

1971

First Screening Program

The first community-based Tay-Sachs screening program launches, targeting Ashkenazi Jewish populations. Volunteers and community leaders mobilize to educate families about carrier testing and genetic counseling.

1980s

Program Expansion

Screening programs expand nationwide with support from Jewish community organizations. Dor Yeshorim begins offering anonymous genetic compatibility testing for Orthodox Jewish communities.

1990s

Technological Advances

DNA-based testing replaces enzyme testing, making screening more accurate and accessible. Prenatal diagnosis becomes widely available for at-risk couples.

2000s

Global Impact

Prevention programs achieve 90% reduction in Tay-Sachs incidence among Jewish populations worldwide. The model is adopted for other genetic diseases.

Today

Continued Success

Modern screening programs continue to prevent Tay-Sachs cases while serving as a model for preventing other genetic diseases in at-risk populations.

Stories of Prevention

Real experiences from families and medical pioneers

"When Karen was pregnant with our second child, we faced an impossible choice. We decided we couldn't bear to parent two dying children. If our baby had Tay-Sachs, we would place the newborn in foster care and never see the child. It was agonizing, but Dr. Kaback's breakthrough testing gave us hope."

— Bob Zeiger, Pediatric Intern (1970)

"I said, 'There's a baby's life in flux and there's a handsome doctor who will meet you in San Diego. Will you take it?' The flight attendant responded, 'Of course.' That blood sample carried across the country led to the first successful newborn Tay-Sachs test."

— Dr. Michael Kaback, Johns Hopkins (1970)

"It was a devastating disease and an enormously powerful experience as a young doctor. Watching children deteriorate like that and not being able to do anything drove us to find a solution through prevention."

— Dr. Michael Kaback, reflecting on his motivation

"The success of carrier screening depends upon its uptake. Within the Jewish community, screening programs have reduced the incidence of Tay-Sachs by more than 90 percent through a combination of scientific advances and volunteer community activism."

— The Forward, 2017
